Assignment Overview

Scenario

You are a data analyst for the California Department of Human Services. The department is considering implementing an algorithmic system to identify communities that should receive priority for social service funding and outreach programs. Your supervisor has asked you to evaluate the quality and reliability of available census data to inform this decision.

Drawing on our Week 2 discussion of algorithmic bias, you need to assess not just what the data shows, but how reliable it is and what communities might be affected by data quality issues.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply dplyr functions to real census data for policy analysis
  • Evaluate data quality using margins of error
  • Connect technical analysis to algorithmic decision-making
  • Identify potential equity implications of data reliability issues
  • Create professional documentation for policy stakeholders

2.2 Data Quality Assessment

Your Task: Calculate margin of error percentages and create reliability categories.

Requirements: - Calculate MOE percentage: (margin of error / estimate) * 100 - Create reliability categories: - High Confidence: MOE < 5% - Moderate Confidence: MOE 5-10%
- Low Confidence: MOE > 10% - Create a flag for unreliable estimates (MOE > 10%)

Hint: Use mutate() with case_when() for the categories.

# Calculate MOE percentage and reliability categories using mutate()
ca_reliability <- ca_clean %>%
  mutate(
    income_moe_pct = round((median_hh_incomeM / median_hh_incomeE) * 100, 2),
    reliability = case_when(
      income_moe_pct < 5 ~ "High Confidence",
      income_moe_pct < 10 ~ "Moderate",
      TRUE ~ "Low Confidence"

Data Quality Commentary:

These results highlight the error and uncertainty of relying on a single data source. For the algorithm as a whole, if it depends only on median household income, it may misclassify counties with small sample sizes. In counties with large income inequality, the median may also fail to reflect the problem.

I found the clearest and most reliable systematic pattern to be that, for sample-based ACS data, statistical reliability tends to decrease as the unit of analysis becomes smaller. For example, when analyzing median household income at the county level, 70.7% of California counties fall into the High Confidence category. However, when the analysis is refined to the census tract level, MOE percentages generally increase. In addition, for Los Angeles County—the most populous county—race/ethnicity estimates are relatively reliable at the county level, with low MOE. But once the analysis is further broken down into individual tracts within the county, many tracts are still classified as High MOE, likely because each tract has a smaller sample size and smaller subgroup counts.

Based on my results, the communities most vulnerable to algorithmic bias are small-population tracts, because their ACS estimates have higher uncertainty and are more likely to be flagged as High MOE. In my tract-level pattern analysis, High MOE tracts also tend to have a higher Black share and a lower Hispanic/Latino share, while the White share differs very little between groups. However, I cannot claim these demographic patterns are direct causal drivers of bias risk. A more defensible interpretation is that algorithmic bias risk increases where the underlying inputs are statistically less reliable—often due to smaller effective sample sizes and smaller subgroup counts at the tract level—so algorithmic bias may be more prone to be seen in those areas.

As the geographic scale becomes smaller (from counties to census tracts), uncertainty in ACS estimates generally increases. This is mainly because sample-based surveys have smaller effective sample sizes in small geographic units, and because some racial/ethnic groups have smaller absolute counts within a tract, which can substantially increase MOE percentages. At the same time, my results show that High MOE is more common in tracts with smaller populations and demographic compositions that differ from the overall average, suggesting that data quality issues and potential bias risk may be more concentrated in specific communities. I also found that ACS data quality may vary across states and counties; for example, Pennsylvania has no counties with High MOE (greater than 10%). Therefore, cross-regional conclusions should be confirmed by running the same tests in additional locations.

When the Department encounters areas with high MOE, it should flag them as unreliable areas rather than directly using ACS data. In practice, the Department can set a clear “High MOE” threshold. When MOE is too high, the data should not be used for fully automated decisions. Instead, the Department should use more robust approaches, such as merging adjacent tracts, analyzing at a larger geographic scale, or validating results with multiple data sources. If high-MOE areas might be underestimated or overlooked by the algorithm, the Department should also add safeguards, such as human review, a minimum service floor, or targeted policy support.
